Job Market Paper

"The Equilibrium Effects of Wildfires: Evidence from the California Housing Market" Draft coming soon
Abstract

Increased wildfire frequency coupled with development in the wildland-urban interface has recently resulted in some of the most destructive wildfires in California’s history. This paper looks at the equilibrium effects of wildfire exposure in the context of the housing market. Unlike other environmental goods typically studied in hedonic work, wildfires directly impact the supply of housing which makes the effect of exposure unclear. Using 20 years of California housing transactions and wildfire data, I implement a stacked event study to estimate the effect of wildfire exposure on housing prices. Prices for properties within the fire perimter decline severely and reach the lowest point of 72% 8 months after the fire. However, this effect isn't permanent, and prices recover to pre-fire levels 3 years after the fire. Large declines in prices of undamaged properties within the fire signal it's not just physical damage that results in lower prices but also worse local neighborhood quality which lowers demand. Moreover, building permit issuances spike immediately after the fire and signal an improvement of housing and neighborhood quality which driving demand and helping prices to recover. Based on the changes in prices after fire exposure, wildfires impact housing markets primarily through changing demand.
